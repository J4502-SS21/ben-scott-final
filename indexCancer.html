Cancer Alley:
A crash course on
a ravaged community.

Cancer Alley is a region in the south of Louisiana that has one of the highest cancer cases in the nation. These cases are due to the massive number of chemical factories surrounding towns, all of which are fairly poor, most of which are predominately African American.

These chemical companies release chemical such as (names) into the air, water and land as waste in order to create products like (names). The state of Louisiana provides tax incentives for companies to build such factories, claiming that these factories will drive up the economy of Louisiana.

However, again these efforts to prop up the state’s economy on industry comes at the expense of Louisianians living in towns surrounded by factories. Towns surrounded by factories often don’t have much of their population employed in those factories. Ownership claims that prospective employees must have proper certifications, which townsfolk often lack.

One town, St. Gabriel, has over 30 chemical factories in a (number) mile radius. These factories often surpass the EPA’s recommend guidelines for the amount of toxic waste a factory should expel for certain chemicals.

Local Louisiana media have consistently reported that the state’s governing body over industrial regulations, the Louisiana Department of Environmental Quality, has not been as stringent in policing the companies in regards to their pollution.

The department has been victim to budget cuts and lacking resources over the years, especially during former Louisiana Governor Bobby Jindal’s administration. This has resulted in a reduced number of employees, a high workload for every employee and frequent employee turnover.

“This is due to a lack of resources, natural disasters and a culture in which the state agency is expected to protect industry – according to the inspector general of the EPA,” one Times-Picayune article wrote about the DEQ.

In collaboration with ProPublica, the Times-Picayune also reported the following:

“The auditor’s report found that the time it took for the LDEQ to issue enforcement actions after a known violation more than doubled between fiscal year 2015 and 2019, from nearly 10 months to nearly 20 months.”

“Auditors also found it could take as long as nine years from the time a company was cited for violating emission standards before it was ordered to pay a fine or was required by a settlement to pay for a mitigation project.”

Air quality has certainly improved since the 1980s. However, that increase in quality must be compared across other states in the country, almost all of which have seen much more drastic increases in quality of air when compared to Louisiana’s increase.

Louisiana ranks bottom in life expectancy in the U.S. The general trend across the country shows that states in the southeastern U.S. have the lowest life expectancy. Notably, a good portion of those states also share a border alongside the Mississippi River, as well.

Louisiana's life expectancy by parish also provides some interesting takeaways. While there is no direct smoking gun, it still shows that Louisiana is fairly consistent across the board regarding life expectancy, save for outliers that have wealthier populations, like St. Tammany Parish, or parishes with tiny populations, like Tensas Parish. However, it is important to note that parish demographics can vary from city to city.
